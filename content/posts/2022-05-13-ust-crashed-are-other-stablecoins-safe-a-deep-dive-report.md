<p>In a matter of days, UST went from $1 to as low as $0.05, dragging otherwise boring stablecoins into the spotlight.</p><p>The demise of UST coincided with (or maybe even contributed to) the overall crypto market crash. While usually, traders try to wait out market sell-offs in stablecoins, this time fears spread to USDT and other coins.</p><p>In this post, we will explore different types of stable coins, see what happened to UST and LUNA, review other risks, and explore alternatives through hedging with derivatives.</p><h2 id="stablecoins-explained">Stablecoins explained</h2><p>Stable coins are cryptocurrencies that aim to maintain a stable exchange rate. They are pegged to fiat currencies, usually the US Dollar.</p><p>In practice, stablecoin prices often slightly deviate from their fiat counterparts. Usually, the price quickly returns to the target value.</p><p>However, if the price moves too far away from the peg, this can cause a loss of faith and spark a panic sell-off that would send the price even lower. To avoid that, stablecoins employ various methods of keeping the peg.</p><h3 id="why-stablecoins-are-stable">Why stablecoins are stable</h3><p>A stablecoin is stable because people <em>believe</em> that it will maintain its price.</p><p>When big traders and market makers see that the price of a USD-linked stable coin is below $1, they can buy it and pocket an easy profit once the price returns to $1. Similarly, when the price is above $1, they can take out a loan to sell the stable coins and buy back when the price drops to $1.</p><p>This confidence comes from the way these stable coins are set up.</p><h3 id="centralized-vs-decentralized-stablecoins">Centralized vs decentralized stablecoins</h3><p>A <a href="https://zerion.io/blog/solana-stablecoins-the-complete-guide/" rel="noreferrer">stablecoin</a> could be centralized or decentralized:</p><ul><li>A centralized stable coin is issued by a single issuer, which mints all new coins</li><li>A decentralized stable coin is minted by interacting with a smart contract</li></ul><p>This distinction isn’t binary or clear-cut.</p><p>A centralized coin can be held by a very wide range of players, who each have different agendas. And founders of decentralized coins can often still have outsized influence even if they can’t control the smart contract.</p><p>Both centralized and decentralized stable coins can be either collateralized or algorithmic.</p><h3 id="collateralized-vs-algorithmic-stablecoins">Collateralized vs algorithmic stablecoins</h3><p>A collateralized stable coin is backed by some assets, known as collateral.</p><p>These assets are held either off-chain on the issuer’s balance sheet (centralized coins) or on-chain (usually decentralized).</p><p>Almost anything could act as collateral:</p><ul><li>USD on a bank account (for USDC)</li><li>US Treasuries (also for USDC)</li><li>Commercial papers (for USDT)</li><li>ETH and other cryptocurrencies, and even other stable coins (for DAI)</li></ul><p>Collateralization is a degree, which means a stable coin can be:</p><ul><li>Overcollateralized (e.g. DAI) — for every stablecoin there is more than $1 in collateral</li><li>Fully collateralized — $1 worth of assets for every stablecoin</li><li>Partially collateralized — under $1 worth of assets for every coin</li></ul><p>Unless the collateral is on-chain, it’s hard to know exactly what backs the coin. Even a financial audit by a reputable firm is only a snapshot of the accounts on a particular date.</p><p>Meanwhile, overcollateralized stables coins like DAI are capital inefficient. To get $1 you must lock up more than $1 worth of assets, which could earn a yield or do something productive.</p><p>That’s why there is a lot of hope for algorithmic stablecoins.</p><p>Algorithmic stablecoins aim to maintain the peg through algorithmically modifying the supply.</p><h2 id="what-is-ust-and-what-happened-to-it">What is UST and what happened to it?</h2><p>UST is an algorithmic decentralized stablecoin, which sought to maintain its peg through algorithmically modifying the supply of its arbitrage token LUNA.</p><p>Both UST and LUNA tokens run on the Terra blockchain, led by its charismatic founder Do Kwon.</p><p>By design, every 1 UST can be exchanged into $1 worth of newly minted LUNA. And when 1 UST is minted, $1 worth of LUNA is burned. This is supposed to stabilize the price of UST.</p><ul><li>So if 1 UST = $1.1, you can buy $1 in LUNA, burn it to mint 1 UST, sell it for USD and instantly make a profit of $0.1.</li><li>And if 1 UST = $0.90, you can exchange UST for $1 worth of LUNA, sell it, and pocket a profit of $0.1.</li></ul><p>At first, this worked well.</p><p>UST became increasingly popular due to a 20% yield offered on Anchor, a lending and borrowing protocol. To mint new UST, people burned LUNA, decreasing supply and pushing its price up. The token climbed the ranks, attracting even more people into the Terra ecosystem who minted more UST.</p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card kg-card-hascaption"><img src="https://zerion.io/blog/content/images/2022/05/Screenshot-2022-05-13-at-18.20.31.png" class="kg-image" alt="UST market cap surged to $18 billion" loading="lazy" width="1634" height="950" srcset="https://zerion.io/blog/content/images/size/w600/2022/05/Screenshot-2022-05-13-at-18.20.31.png 600w, https://zerion.io/blog/content/images/size/w1000/2022/05/Screenshot-2022-05-13-at-18.20.31.png 1000w, https://zerion.io/blog/content/images/size/w1600/2022/05/Screenshot-2022-05-13-at-18.20.31.png 1600w, https://zerion.io/blog/content/images/2022/05/Screenshot-2022-05-13-at-18.20.31.png 1634w" sizes="(min-width: 720px) 720px"><figcaption><span style="white-space: pre-wrap;">UST market cap surged to $18 billion</span></figcaption></figure><p>In less than 2 years, the market cap of LUNA rose from $5 billion to $40 billion at its peak in April 2022. During the same period, the market cap of UST went from $1.7 billion to $18 billion.</p><p>Then things reversed.</p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card kg-card-hascaption"><img src="https://zerion.io/blog/content/images/2022/05/Screenshot-2022-05-13-at-18.10.08.png" class="kg-image" alt="UST lost its peg and the death spiral started" loading="lazy" width="1662" height="954" srcset="https://zerion.io/blog/content/images/size/w600/2022/05/Screenshot-2022-05-13-at-18.10.08.png 600w, https://zerion.io/blog/content/images/size/w1000/2022/05/Screenshot-2022-05-13-at-18.10.08.png 1000w, https://zerion.io/blog/content/images/size/w1600/2022/05/Screenshot-2022-05-13-at-18.10.08.png 1600w, https://zerion.io/blog/content/images/2022/05/Screenshot-2022-05-13-at-18.10.08.png 1662w" sizes="(min-width: 720px) 720px"><figcaption><span style="white-space: pre-wrap;">UST lost its peg and the death spiral started</span></figcaption></figure><p>Here’s what happened with UST and LUNA.</p><ol><li><strong>May 7-8. </strong>Some whale trader sells $300 million of UST on Curve — exactly when $150 million in liquidity was being moved from 3pool to 4pool (likely not a coincidence). The <a href="https://dune.com/queries/685450/1271567">pool becomes heavily imbalanced</a> and UST’s peg breaks to about $0.98. (Here's a refresher on how <a href="https://zerion.io/blog/a-deep-dive-into-liquidity-pools/">liquidity pools</a> work if you need it).</li><li>Investors begin to leave Anchor. Some of them sell UST to redeem it for LUNA. This pushes the price even lower to $0.97.</li><li><strong>May 9.</strong> Someone sells $650 million of UST on Binance, pushing the price as low as $0.62.</li><li><strong>May 10.</strong> More people flee Anchor, and deposits drop by almost 85%. Because there is no liquidity anywhere, people redeem UST for LUNA, flooding the market and crashing the price of LUNA.</li><li><strong>May 11. </strong>Terra founder Do Kwon <a href="https://twitter.com/stablekwon/status/1524331171189956609">addresses the community</a>, admitting that “the only path forward will be to absorb the stablecoin supply that wants to exit” (i.e. let people sell at under $1).</li><li><strong>May 12. </strong>Terra’s blockchain becomes overloaded and <a href="https://twitter.com/terra_money/status/1524935730308456448?s=21&amp;t=9VDH9iHl-sVn4vC92-WvRw">is halted</a>. Anchor’s borrowers get liquidated, and their collateral is sold.</li><li><strong>May 13</strong>. LUNA and UST enter a death spiral, where depreciating UST is constantly redeemed for freefalling LUNA, which is down from the all-time high of $119 to $0.0000009 (-99.999%).</li></ol><p>Overall, UST’s algorithm worked as intended. However, it was not enough to restore the peg once confidence was lost and the downward spiral started.</p><p>This doesn’t mean that the same fate awaits other stablecoins, but it’s important to understand what risks exist in these seemingly safe tokens.</p><h2 id="stablecoins-risks">Stablecoins risks</h2><p>Stablecoins are not risk-free. Nothing is, especially in crypto. Let’s review some of the key risks.</p><h3 id="%F0%9F%93%89-market-risks">📉 Market risks</h3><p>Big market movements, even if they are not directly related to the particular stable coin, can put too much pressure on the peg.</p><p>If everybody is swapping into a particular stable coin, the price could exceed $1 (that’s what happened with USDC this week).</p><p>And if everybody is dumping a stablecoin to join the bull run, the price could be pushed to under $1.</p><h3 id="%F0%9F%92%B0-quality-of-collateral">💰 Quality of collateral</h3><p>If assets that back the stablecoin deteriorate, people could start selling the stablecoin.</p><p>FUD (fears, doubts, uncertainties) alone could have a significant price impact. For off-chain assets, it’s hard to know the true quality of what's on the issuer's balance. The USDT’s collateral (or the lack of it) has long been the subject of heated debates.</p><h3 id="%F0%9F%A4%96-algorithm-risks">🤖 Algorithm risks</h3><p>Any algorithmic coin depends on a number of assumptions.</p><p>Most importantly, these coins assume that people would behave in a certain way. And that can only be tested in extreme conditions of the real market. </p><h3 id="%F0%9F%8F%9B%EF%B8%8F-regulatory-risks">🏛️ Regulatory risks</h3><p>Today, stablecoins are largely unregulated. But that is likely to change.</p><p>The <a href="https://finance.yahoo.com/news/regulatory-woes-forces-facebook-sell-162812966.html#:~:text=Meta%20(Facebook)%20Sells%20Diem%20to%20Silvergate&amp;text=Notably%2C%20the%20Diem%20Association%20had,the%20operations%20of%20the%20organization.&amp;text=According%20to%20the%20Association%2C%20the,the%20project%20couldn't%20progress.">regulators in the US killed Diem</a>, Facebook’s ambitious stablecoin project. Existing stablecoins are much smaller in scale than what Facebook envisioned but if and when they get bigger, regulators will likely start paying attention.</p><h3 id="%F0%9F%92%A7-liquidity-risk">💧 Liquidity risk</h3><p>This risk materializes when everybody tries to sell and there are not enough buyers.</p><p>Issuers and their backers of centralized coins can pick up excessive sell pressure. Decentralized coins, however, must rely on holders’ self-interest.</p><h2 id="a-list-of-major-stablecoins">A list of major stablecoins</h2><p>Now that we explored different types of stablecoins and various risks, we can look at how major coins performed.
