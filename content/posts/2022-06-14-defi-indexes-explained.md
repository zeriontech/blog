<blockquote>DeFi indexes are the easiest way to get passive diversification for your crypto portfolio</blockquote><p>Against the backdrop of an explosion of DeFi tokens and structured financial products, decentralized index funds, or DeFi indexes, have emerged as an easy way to get passive, diversified exposure to the market.</p><div class="kg-card kg-callout-card kg-callout-card-blue"><div class="kg-callout-emoji">💡</div><div class="kg-callout-text">First, install <a href="https://link.zerion.io/H0VRH6UIErb" rel="noreferrer">Zerion Wallet</a>, the perfect companion for DeFi indexes.</div></div><h3 id="defi-indexes-are-similar-to-traditional-etfs">DeFi indexes are similar to traditional ETFs</h3><p><strong>What is an index?</strong></p><p>Say you want to eat a fruit salad.</p><p>There are two ways of going about that: you can either buy each individual fruit, chop them up and attempt to create a snack that tastes good… Or you can buy a fruit salad that has already been assembled by a vendor who balanced ingredients in order to make it just right. An index is like a fruit salad.</p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card kg-card-hascaption"><img src="https://zerion.io/blog/content/images/2022/07/DeFi-indexes-explained.png" class="kg-image" alt="" loading="lazy" width="512" height="146"><figcaption><span style="white-space: pre-wrap;">Source: </span><i><em class="italic" style="white-space: pre-wrap;">What is an index, by Phuture</em></i></figcaption></figure><p>In traditional finance, the closest analogy to a decentralized index fund is an <strong>exchange traded fund (ETF)</strong> which tracks the performance of an index, sector, commodity or specific asset.</p><p>ETFs can be <strong>passive</strong> or <strong>active</strong>. For example, the Vanguard S&amp;P 500 ETF is a passive equity basket of 500 large companies that are publicly traded on US markets. On the other hand, active ETFs usually have a team making decisions on the underlying portfolio allocation. These kinds of ETFs have been gaining steady attention for outperforming their passive counterparts, pioneered by the likes of ARK Invest.</p><h3 id="differences-between-etfs-and-defi-indexes">Differences between ETFs and DeFi indexes</h3><p>While there are strong similarities between ETFs and DeFi indexes, there are some fundamental differences too.</p><h4 id="set-up-costs">Set-up costs</h4><p>Launching an ETF is a costly business and comes with hefty regulatory obstacles. DeFi indexes can be set up relatively quickly because they’re built on permissionless technology and use smart contracts to manage portfolio composition.</p><p>For example, indexed.finance’s Degen Index took just 4 weeks to launch after a community member proposed the idea. This highlights how DeFi dramatically reduces the barriers to creating and participating in new financial products.</p><h4 id="management">Management</h4><p>Regardless of whether a DeFi index follows an active or a passive strategy, portfolio composition is a largely automated process. For example, a simple smart contract might swap an index’s underlying stablecoin for one that offers a higher interest rate. A more complex index could be run on a rebase smart contract, where the supply of the index is a function of its price.</p><p>In DeFi, index management and rebalancing are transparent and automatic, so user’s funds are always following the index's initial strategy. DeFi index funds proactively communicate how the index is composed and it is rebalanced going forward. An example can be found in the documentation for the Phuture DeFi Index, PDI.</p><h4 id="bonus-yields">Bonus yields</h4><p>Most DeFi indexes are managed by DAOs who use liquidity mining as an incentive to bolster market liquidity and as a way to distribute governance tokens. When you stake index tokens, you’re eligible to earn further rewards.</p><p>DeFi’s composability also allows for index funds to boost their returns by utilizing DeFi’s yield-generating protocols. The first DeFi index to do so is PDI, using Yearn’s Vaults. Additional returns are always good for investors, no matter if it’s a bull or bear market.</p><h3 id="what-to-look-for-in-a-defi-index">What to look for in a DeFi index</h3><p>A straightforward way to compare DeFi indexes is to look at price performance and market cap. You can do this with <a href="https://zerion.io/">Zerion Wallet</a>’s Index page by toggling between columns.</p><p>It’s also important to understand how DeFi indexes are structured and managed, known as the <strong>index methodology</strong>. Differences in weighting, rebalance methods and other factors can mean that two indexes with the same underlying tokens produce different returns.</p><ul><li><strong>Construction:</strong> Which tokens are eligible to be included in the index, and why?</li><li><strong>Weighting:</strong> How are tokens in the index weighted relative to one another?</li><li><strong>Rebalancing:</strong> How and when are tokens added and deleted from the index?</li><li><strong>Portfolio structure:</strong> What kind of contract is used to operate the index?*</li></ul><p>Not all indexes represent a basket of underlying assets held in a smart contract. For example, <strong>synthetic indexes</strong> do not actually hold any underlying tokens, but rather use price feeds to track their value such as Synthetix’s sDeFi.</p><p><strong>Liquidity pool indexes</strong> offer both diversified exposure to a basket of assets and the ability to earn trading fees. The downside is that index holders can also be exposed to impermanent loss, like any <a href="https://zerion.io/blog/a-deep-dive-into-liquidity-pools/">liquidity pool</a>. For example, PowerPool indexes are built as Balancer pools and Indexed Finance uses a similar model forked from Balancer.</p><p>Finally, investors should also consider fees and staking opportunities (liquidity mining) as these will affect net returns.
