<p>If you spent any time interacting with <a href="https://zerion.io/blog/how-to-invest-in-defi/">DeFi</a> or <a href="https://zerion.io/blog/how-to-make-buy-and-sell-nfts/">NFTs</a> on Ethereum, you likely encountered infamously high gas fees and slow transactions.</p><p>That's the cost of decentralization. </p><p>By design, blockchain and other distributed ledger technologies are slower and more expensive than centralized alternatives. Yet today some of the world's best minds are working on various scaling solutions to make cryptocurrencies faster and more affordable for everyday users.</p><p>In this article, we'll explore cryptocurrency scaling solutions, covering popular Layer 2 networks and answering the most common questions.</p><h2 id="what-are-cryptocurrency-layer-2-scaling-solutions">What are cryptocurrency Layer 2 scaling solutions?</h2><p>Since the inception of Bitcoin in 2009, blockchain technology has offered significant benefits. </p><p>Decentralization, transparent record-keeping, and security are among the most discussed advantages. Blockchain technology has launched a thriving cryptocurrency ecosystem that is constantly pushing the boundaries with use cases that range from finance and currencies to identity and governance. </p><p>Despite this growth, scalability has consistently been a major issue for many blockchain networks. When the volume of pending transactions exceeds the available capacity of the blockchain at a given moment, we experience scaling issues: transactions are slow or just never happen and you need to pay higher fees. </p><p>In an ideal world, any blockchain could accommodate an infinite number of transactions per second (TPS), commonly called throughput. The Bitcoin main chain, however, can only manage 3 to 7 TPS. VisaNet, Visa’s centralized electronic payment network, can process around 20,000 TPS. </p><p>The difference in TPS is due to the decentralization that Bitcoin and other blockchains aim to achieve. </p><p>To replace a basic centralized system, you need a lot of time and processing power. In a decentralized system, instead of a single database, a global network of nodes accepts, distributes, and validates each transaction.</p><p>Layer 2 scaling solutions seek to increase the throughput while maintaining decentralization. By building on top of the existing blockchain (the Layer 1), Layer 2 solutions aim to: </p><ul><li>Increase the speed of transactions, </li><li>Increase transaction throughput (the number of transactions per second), </li><li>Decrease transaction fees. </li></ul><p>While there are always some tradeoffs, Layer 2 scaling solutions could potentially move the blockchain community closer to realizing its common goal of making cryptocurrencies and blockchain-based systems accessible to everyone in a secure, convenient, and efficient manner.</p><h2 id="what-are-layer-2-solutions-and-what-are-they-for">What are Layer 2 solutions and what are they for?</h2><p>Layer 2 refers to a network or technology that runs on top of a blockchain protocol to help it scale and be more efficient.</p><p>Blockchains, such as Ethereum, have increased in popularity in recent years due to their programmability (smart contracts) and resistance to censorship, allowing a wide range of businesses and use cases to be developed on top of them. </p><p>However, Ethereum can only process 7 to 11 transactions per second, compared to over 20,000 transactions per second on the Visa network.</p><p>As the blockchain gets overburdened, users compete to have their transactions completed as quickly as possible. This promotes a bidding war for space in each block, causing transaction prices to skyrocket. It cost over $80 to send a token to another address on the Ethereum network at one point in 2021.</p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card kg-card-hascaption"><img src="https://zerion.io/blog/content/images/2022/04/chart--3-.png" class="kg-image" alt="Average transaction fee on Ethereum" loading="lazy" width="1200" height="1100" srcset="https://zerion.io/blog/content/images/size/w600/2022/04/chart--3-.png 600w, https://zerion.io/blog/content/images/size/w1000/2022/04/chart--3-.png 1000w, https://zerion.io/blog/content/images/2022/04/chart--3-.png 1200w" sizes="(min-width: 720px) 720px"><figcaption>Average transaction fee on Ethereum</figcaption></figure><p>Layer 2 solutions aim to alleviate the burden on Ethereum and to overcome the bottleneck problem outlined above. </p><p>Layer 2 solutions separate transactions from the underlying blockchain, allowing for the processing of thousands of transactions per second. <!--kg-card-end: markdown--><h2 id="bitcoin-layer-2-scaling-solutions">Bitcoin Layer 2 scaling solutions</h2><p>As the first cryptocurrency, Bitcoin was also the first to experience scaling issues.</p><p>At the peak of the 2017 bull market, the Bitcoin average transaction fees exceeded $45. During the 2021 bull run, the average fee reached $65. </p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card kg-card-hascaption"><img src="https://zerion.io/blog/content/images/2022/04/Screenshot-2022-04-27-at-08.59.15.png" class="kg-image" alt="Average Bitcoin transaction fee" loading="lazy" width="1420" height="520" srcset="https://zerion.io/blog/content/images/size/w600/2022/04/Screenshot-2022-04-27-at-08.59.15.png 600w, https://zerion.io/blog/content/images/size/w1000/2022/04/Screenshot-2022-04-27-at-08.59.15.png 1000w, https://zerion.io/blog/content/images/2022/04/Screenshot-2022-04-27-at-08.59.15.png 1420w" sizes="(min-width: 720px) 720px"><figcaption>Average Bitcoin transaction fee</figcaption></figure><p>One of the most well-known Layer 2 solutions for Bitcoin is the <strong>Bitcoin Lightning Network, </strong>which was first proposed in 2015. </p><p>This Layer 2 solution creates payment channels between two parties and only updates the balance privately among themselves. Once the channel is closed, the final balances are published to the main net Bitcoin blockchain. The Lightning Network also adds some smart contract capabilities to Bitcoin, which is a significant upgrade to the network as a whole.</p><h3 id="pros">Pros</h3><ul><li><strong>Faster <strong><strong>Transactions</strong></strong>: </strong>Bitcoin has a transaction rate of about seven transactions per second (TPS). Furthermore, Bitcoin keeps track of transactions in ten-minute blocks. After the addition of six blocks, which takes almost an hour, you will know that your transaction has been successfully recorded onchain. Instead, transactions within the Lightning payment channel are nearly instantaneous. </li><li><strong><strong><strong>Enhanced </strong></strong>P<strong><strong>rivacy</strong></strong>: </strong>The Lightning transaction is only recorded on the public blockchain when the payment channel is eventually closed and the amount is distributed to both parties. Unlike onchain payments, transactions that happen within the channel are not visible to the public.<strong> </strong></li><li><strong><strong><strong>Transaction Costs are Reduced</strong></strong>: </strong>The Lightning Network allows transactions to be made for as little as 0.00000001 BTC ($0.01). The Lightning Network has effectively reduced transaction costs to one satoshi (1 SAT = 1/100,000,000 BTC).</li></ul><h3 id="cons">Cons</h3><ul><li><strong><strong><strong>Risks of Offline Transactions</strong></strong>: </strong>On the Lightning Network, going offline has its own set of issues. While you're away, the other party can close the channel and pocket payments. Fraudulent Channel Close is the term for this.</li><li><strong>Online V<strong><strong>ulnera</strong></strong>bility: </strong>Because all parties involved in the transaction must be online and sign with their private keys, the whole wallet could be compromised if the computer storing the private keys is hacked.</li><li><strong><strong><strong>Malicious Attacks</strong></strong>: </strong>Congestion caused by a malicious attack is another network risk. Participants may not be able to get their money back quickly enough if the payment channels become congested as a result of a malicious hack or attack.</li></ul><p>Now that we explored the most popular scaling solution for Bitcoin, let's see what approaches are used to scale Ethereum. </p><h2 id="types-of-layer-2-scaling-solutions">Types of Layer 2 scaling solutions</h2><p>Two of the most discussed Layer 2 scaling solutions for Ethereum are two types of rollups: <strong>zero-knowledge rollups</strong> and <strong>optimistic rollups</strong>.  </p><p>Rollups are Layer 2 blockchain scaling solutions that perform transactions outside of the Layer 1 blockchain and then post the data from the transactions to the Layer 2 blockchain. Rollups rely on Layer 1 for security.</p><p><strong>Pros</strong></p><ul><li>Rollups help to increase transaction throughput.</li><li>The Layer 1 chain stores all transaction data, making it secure and decentralized.</li><li>Rollups reduce gas fees for users.</li><li>Since Optimistic rollups are EVM and Solidity compatible, you can do there anything you can on Ethereum Layer 1.</li></ul><p><strong>Cons</strong></p><ul><li>Due to the risk of fraud, onchain transactions have relatively long wait times. </li><li>A transaction's order can be influenced by the network operator<strong>.</strong></li><li>Greater centralization as teams that run rollup projects typically have control over smart contracts.</li></ul><h3 id="zero-knowledge-rollups">Zero-knowledge rollups</h3><p>ZK-rollups (Zero-knowledge rollups) are Ethereum smart contracts that scale the network by executing numerous transfers outside the primary blockchain (off-chain).</p><p>ZK-rollups aggregate or "roll up" hundreds of off-chain transactions into a single transaction. This transaction is then returned to the main chain (the Layer 1 Ethereum) with a cryptographic "zero-knowledge" proof of validity. <a href="https://hackernoon.com/eli5-zero-knowledge-proof-78a276db9eff">Zero knowledge proof</a> is essentially a way of proving that you know some information without actually revealing that information. </p><p>Because most data is stored off-chain and only the proof goes to the main chain, the ZK-rollups Layer 2 scaling approach outperforms Layer 1 Ethereum in terms of transaction speed and throughput. And because there is less competition for block space, gas prices fall and transactions become less expensive.</p><h3 id="optimistic-rollups">Optimistic rollups</h3><p>An optimistic rollup is a Layer 2 scaling solution that moves most transaction processing off-chain and only posts a summary of these transactions to the main net Ethereum. This helps to save up on gas fees. </p><p>The system updates the "state" of the rollup on the main Ethereum blockchain by publishing a <a href="https://www.investopedia.com/terms/m/merkle-root-cryptocurrency.asp">Merkle root</a> of the transactions that occur within the rollup.</p><p>Before the state is updated, a network of external validators validates the Merkle roots to ensure they are correct. If there is any inconsistency, the validator can publish a fraud-proof during the dispute period, causing the system's state to be rolled back to the previous valid state.</p><p>The main difference between optimistic rollups and ZK-rollups is that optimistic Layer 2 transactions take longer. Before the state can be updated, optimistic rollups must rely on external validators to check the Merkle roots. However, optimistic rollups have the advantage of supporting smart contracts in the same way as the underlying smart contracts blockchain does.</p><p>Despite their recent prominence, ZK-rollups and Optimistic rollups are not the only scaling solutions for Ethereum.</p><h3 id="nested-blockchain-plasma">Nested blockchain: Plasma</h3><p>Plasma is an approach to scaling Ethereum by creating additional nested blockchains, which connect to the main net. </p><p>Also known as 'sidechains' or 'child chains', these additional blockchains can be designed to specific needs and offload most transactions from the main chain (also known as 'root chain'). Each sidechain does all the computations and only submits the minimum data to the main chain. However, any disputes could be settled on the main chain. This delegation greatly increases the overall bandwidth of all blockchains. </p><p>Finally, each side chain can have additional child chains, branching out like a tree.</p><p><strong>Pros</strong></p><ul><li>This solution not only reduces the load in the root chain but also increases scalability exponentially if properly implemented.</li><li>Each child chain can be tailored to specific needs such as micropayments or computation-heavy applications. </li><li>Plasma is compatible with other scaling solutions such as different block sizes or sharding.</li></ul><p><strong>Cons</strong></p><ul><li>To allow for disputes, withdrawals are postponed for several days. Liquidity providers can mitigate this, but there is a capital cost associated with this.</li><li>To ensure the security of your funds, you must periodically monitor the network (liveness requirement) or delegate this responsibility to someone else.</li><li>Increased centralization as Plasma counts on an operator to store and serve off-chain data when needed.</li></ul><h3 id="what-is-a-sidechain">What is a sidechain?</h3><p>A sidechain is a blockchain ledger that is linked to the main chain of a cryptocurrency, such as Bitcoin or Ethereum. </p><p>Sidechains could be viewed as a cross between Layer 1 and Layer 2 scaling solutions. They do not create an alternative Layer 1 blockchain but instead run in addition to the main blockchain (also known as the native layer). Sidechains are linked by two-way bridges, which allow cryptocurrency transfers from the main chain to a sidechain that requires some third-party trust.</p><p>Sidechain typically has an independent consensus mechanism from the native layer. To improve scalability and processing speed, the mechanism can be tweaked. The mainchain must still confirm transaction records, maintain security, and resolve disputes in this situation.</p><p><strong><a href="https://liquid.net/">The Liquid Network</a></strong>, which is linked to Bitcoin's main chain, is an example of a sidechain. Like Layer 2 solutions, it aims to address scalability issues by unloading some of the confirmation and transaction processing processes to another network. This allows the main chain to handle a greater volume of transactions.</p><p><strong>Pros</strong></p><ul><li>Sidechains are a well-established technology.</li><li>It is compatible with the Ethereum virtual machine (EVM) and supports general computation.</li></ul><p><strong>Cons</strong></p><ul><li>A sidechain is often less decentralized<strong>. </strong>It has its own consensus mechanism, which might not be fraud-proof.</li></ul><h3 id="what-is-a-parachain">What is a parachain?</h3><p>The term "parachain" refers to a "parallel chain" that connects to the main relay chain. These chains run parallel to one another in a network of interconnected blockchains. </p><p>Parachains are usually all built within the same framework, so they have the same security features, and they are all linked to the central relay chain. They can, however, have different specifications to address their respective applications. </p><p><strong><a href="https://polkadot.network/parachains/">Polkadot</a></strong> is based on this concept of relay and parachains. Because the distribution is spread to handle workloads, parachains can enable very fast transactions.</p><h3 id="what-is-a-hashgraph">What is a hashgraph?</h3><p>Hashgraph is not a scaling solution but is a separate distributed ledger technology described as a blockchain alternative. </p><p><strong><a href="https://hedera.com/">Hedera Hashgraph</a></strong> is the only ledger that uses hashgraph at the moment, as the technology is patented. Leemon Baird invented hashgraph and it is the intellectual property of the Swirlds Corporation, which Baird founded.</p><p>Hashgraphs, unlike blockchains, do not use miners to validate transactions. Instead, hashgraphs employ a "gossip about gossip" protocol in which individual network nodes "gossip" about transactions to generate directed acyclic graphs that time-sequence transactions without grouping them into blocks. Each "gossip" message includes one or more transactions, as well as a date, a digital signature, and the cryptographic hashes of two previous occurrences.</p><p>A hashgraph can handle thousands of transactions per second, whereas Bitcoin allows for about 5 transactions per second and Ethereum allows for about 11 transactions per second.</p><p>Yet Ethereum is constantly evolving and its throughput is set to increase if it would successfully execute its 'ETH2' upgrade.  </p><h2 id="what-is-ethereum-20">What is Ethereum 2.0?</h2><p>Ethereum 2 (ETH2) is an upgrade that aims to improve the network's security and scalability. This upgrade included a transition from Ethereum's mining model ("Proof of Work") to a staking model ("Proof of Stake"). This move is known as <a href="https://zerion.io/blog/the-merge/">the Merge</a>.</p><p>This set of improvements will increase Ethereum's scalability and put it on par with other leading blockchains in terms of throughput (TPS). </p><p>As part of this move, ETH holders can stake their <a href="https://zerion.io/blog/crypto-coins-vs-tokens-the-difference-explained/">coins</a> to earn rewards for their contributions to validation efforts. To stake coins on their own, they need a minimum of 32 ETH and lock up the coins until ETH2 launches. However, some <a href="https://zerion.io/blog/how-to-stake-eth-lido-vs-rocket-pool-vs-stakewise/">liquid staking protocols</a> let holders stake any amount of ETH while also keeping liquidity. </p><p>Ahead of the upgrade, Ethereum also underwent a code change known as the <a href="https://notes.ethereum.org/@vbuterin/eip-1559-faq">Ethereum improvement proposal 1559</a> (EIP-1559) in August of 2021 to improve transaction fee pricing. </p><p>The cost for a transaction is now calculated using EIP-1599 instead of the first-price auction process. There is a base fee for all transactions to be included in the next block in the EIP-1599 update, as well as a priority fee (tips for miners) that speed up transaction processing. The base fee is set algorithmically depending on network congestion and is then burned. The priority fee (tips) is set by the user.</p><p>For each transaction, the user submits a fee that is higher than the base fee. Users can set a cost cap because the base fee varies depending on network congestion. Users only pay the difference between the final base fee and the fee cap after it is included.</p><p>While EIP-1559 somewhat improved the speed of including the transaction, it's still far from solving scalability issues. For now, Layer 2 solutions for Ethereum are in demand. </p><h2 id="ethereum-layer-2-scaling-solutions">Ethereum Layer 2 scaling solutions</h2><p>Given the great diversity of scaling solutions, there are many projects that aim to scale Ethereum by creating Layer 2 networks. </p><p>Let's briefly review some of the most prominent projects.  </p><h3 id="polygon">Polygon</h3><p>Polygon, formerly known as Matic, is an up-and-coming platform for creating and connecting Ethereum-based blockchain networks. </p><p>Polygon contains a range of tools and components for developers to create optimized Ethereum instances that function as sidechains. This type of scaling solution involves offloading a portion of a blockchain protocol's transactional burden to another system, which then takes care of the majority of the network's processing and only reports back to the main blockchain to complete the process.</p><h3 id="what-is-bitcoin-layer-2">What is Bitcoin Layer 2?</h3><p>Bitcoin Layer 2 refers to various protocols that are built on top of Layer 1 (i.e. Bitcoin) to improve the functionality of the original blockchain. Layer 2 protocols frequently employ off-chain processing elements to address the Layer 1 scaling network's speed and cost inefficiencies. Lightning Network and Liquid Network are two examples of Layer 2 platforms for Bitcoin.</p><h3 id="what-is-layer-1-in-crypto">What is Layer 1 in Crypto?</h3><p>A Layer 1 is a new blockchain that introduces some changes to improve an earlier blockchain.
