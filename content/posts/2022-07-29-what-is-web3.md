<p>Web 3.0 or web3 is how many startups and VCs describe the next version of the internet.</p><p>Unlike the current web dominated by Big Tech, Web3 aims to bring back the decentralization of the early Internet while giving greater privacy, security, and ownership to the people.</p><p>Some people, however, dismiss Web 3.0 as just a buzzword that aims to rebrand crypto. So, who is right? </p><p>In this post, we will explain what Web 3.0 is, how its technology has evolved, what projects are building this new Internet, and how you can get involved.</p>
<!--kg-card-begin: html-->
<h2 id="Definition-of-Web-3.0">Definition of Web 3.0</h2>
<!--kg-card-end: html-->
<p>There is no widely accepted definition of Web 3.0 (or web3 as it's more commonly spelled). Different people put different ideas into this term.</p><p>Originally, Web 3.0 referred to the so-called “Semantic Web”, which seeks to make the Internet machine-readable. Coined by Tim Berners-Lee, the inventor of the World Wide Web, the Semantic Web is “a web of data that can be processed directly and indirectly by machines". Instead of using HTML, the proponents of the Semantic Web want to use new languages specifically tailored to data.</p><p>Web3 (also spelled with the lower case as web3) isn’t concerned about languages or machine readability.</p><p>Instead, Web3 refers to a decentralized version of the internet. In 2014, <a href="https://www.wired.com/story/web3-gavin-wood-interview/">Gavin Wood</a>, one of the co-founders of Ethereum and the creator of Polkadot, coined the term Web3. Several years later he founded the Web3 Foundation to create “a decentralized and fair internet where users control their own data, identity, and destiny”.</p><p>This concept of ownership in Web3 is possible thanks to crypto.</p><h3 id="cryptocurrency-and-blockchain">Cryptocurrency and blockchain</h3><p>There is a simple rule of thumb to check if you’re interacting with Web3.</p><p>If on a web page you see a ‘connect wallet’ button — that’s likely Web3.</p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card kg-card-hascaption"><a href="https://app.zerion.io/connect-wallet"><img src="https://zerion.io/blog/content/images/2022/07/Screenshot-2022-07-29-at-11.36.32.png" class="kg-image" alt="Web 3.0 example" loading="lazy" width="1262" height="486" srcset="https://zerion.io/blog/content/images/size/w600/2022/07/Screenshot-2022-07-29-at-11.36.32.png 600w, https://zerion.io/blog/content/images/size/w1000/2022/07/Screenshot-2022-07-29-at-11.36.32.png 1000w, https://zerion.io/blog/content/images/2022/07/Screenshot-2022-07-29-at-11.36.32.png 1262w" sizes="(min-width: 720px) 720px"></a><figcaption><span style="white-space: pre-wrap;">Web 3.0 example: you can use any wallet to connect to Zerion's web app</span></figcaption></figure><p>Instead of entering a login and password, you connect your <a href="https://zerion.io/blog/custodial-vs-non-custodial-wallet-wallets-comparison-zerion-io/">non-custodial crypto wallet</a> to interact with that website. Your wallet’s cryptographic signature verifies your identity without relying on a centralized database owned by Facebook or Google.</p><p>In a centralized Web 2.0 application, your account is essentially a record in a database. Your account can have a lot of data (try downloading your Facebook data and you’ll be shocked). But all this data is owned by the application’s operator.</p><p>In a decentralized Web 3.0 application, your account is an address on a blockchain such as Ethereum. This address can hold cryptocurrencies, tokens, NFTs, and has the full history of past interactions with other addresses and applications. On Ethereum, this account is public but it could remain anonymous and you’re the only one who can control it.</p><p>This shift from siloed accounts to user-owned accounts is a big deal.</p><p>In fact, decentralization can create a whole set of new applications, including rich metaverses.</p><h3 id="interoperability-of-web-30-and-the-metaverse">Interoperability of Web 3.0 and the metaverse</h3><p>A <a href="https://zerion.io/blog/what-is-the-metaverse-and-why-is-everyone-talking-about-it/">metaverse</a> is a persistent virtual world where people can interact with each other.</p><p>While the media often portrays metaverses as something futuristic, it already exists. Games like Fortnite and Roblox <em>are</em> metaverses even though they have rather narrow audiences.</p><p>These metaverses are also controlled by their developers. </p><p>Epic Games, the developer of Fortnite, has full control over the game universe, including player accounts. The skins for which many players pay real dollars are still owned by Epic Games, not the players.</p><p>Web3 can change that.</p><p>As we discussed, your <a href="https://zerion.io/">crypto wallet</a> can hold tokens and NFTs. These NFTs can represent items in one metaverse. If you buy that NFT and hold it in your wallet, nobody can take it from you. Only you can transfer or sell it.</p><p>This can unlock interoperability between different metaverses. NFTs that have some functions in one metaverse could also be used in another metaverse.</p><p>If all this sounds far-fetched, a glance at the history of the Web can help to see the future trajectory.</p>

<h2 id="Evolution-of-the-Web-3.0-technologies">Evolution of the Web 3.0 technologies</h2>
<!--kg-card-end: html-->
<p>The Internet has developed in several distinct stages.</p><h3 id="web-10-1989-2005-read-only">Web 1.0 (1989-2005): read-only</h3><p>The early Internet was truly decentralized.</p><p>Anybody had the freedom to do anything they wanted as long as it was done within the common open-source protocols: TCP, IP, HTTP, SMTP. We all still use these protocols.</p><p>However, as a user, you didn’t have much to do on the Internet.</p><p>Most early websites were passive. You could come and read. Even adding comments was rare.</p><h3 id="web-20-2005-present-read-and-write">Web 2.0 (2005-present): read and write</h3><p>In the early 2000s, the Internet became more complex.</p><p>The first social networks like MySpace let everyday users create web pages, publish content, and interact with each other. </p><p>These new capabilities unleashed a wave of innovation that spurred social media, complex web applications, ecommerce, and multi-billion dollar companies.</p><p>The proliferation of user-generated content also created natural monopolies. All the Facebook posts, YouTube videos, and Amazon reviews live in databases owned by Big Tech. And these mountains of data help big companies to create new products that lead people to spend even more time creating data.</p><p>Web 2.0 led to several problems.</p><ol><li><strong>User data is centralized:</strong> All your data exists on centralized servers and can be abused, hacked, or leaked.</li><li><strong>User data is not portable: </strong>You can’t easily move your content or audience with you. If TikTok is banned in your country, you need to manually export your videos and somehow ask your followers to switch to a new platform. </li><li><strong>User data is sold:</strong> If you don’t pay for the product, you are the product. Facebook and Google used your data to create an enormous advertising duopoly.</li></ol><h3 id="web-30-emerging-now-read-write-own">Web 3.0 (emerging now): read, write, own</h3><p>What if you could own your data?</p><p>Web3 aims to do that by replacing centralized databases with blockchain technology. Instead of dwelling on corporate servers, your data could sit in your crypto wallet as NFTs.</p><p>The Web 3.0 movement seeks to solve the problems created by Big Tech’s dominance in Web 2.0.</p><ol><li><strong>User data is owned by users:</strong> The data exists as assets on blockchains and other decentralized technologies.</li><li><strong>User data is portable: </strong>Only you control your data through your Web3 wallet.</li><li><strong>User has full control over data: </strong>Some apps might let the user monetize their own data. Other apps might request payments but ideally, this should be explicit and clear.</li></ol><p>The new decentralized web is still more of a vision as the stack of Web 3.0 technologies is not fully formed yet.</p><h2 id="web-30-technologies">Web 3.0 technologies</h2><p>From the technology perspective, Web3 does not seek to fully replace the stack that powers Web 2.0.</p><p>Instead, Web3 can introduce decentralization where it matters. <p>There are several other important elements of the Web3 tech stack, including node infrastructure providers such as <a href="https://www.alchemy.com/">Alchemy</a> and off-chain indexing solutions such as The Graph. If you want to get a deeper understanding of how this decentralized stack works, Preethi Kasireddy prepared a very useful <a href="https://www.preethikasireddy.com/post/the-architecture-of-a-web-3-0-application">guide on Web3 app architecture</a>.</p><p>It’s more important, however, to understand the key features that these technologies enable.</p><h3 id="key-features-of-web-30">Key features of Web 3.0</h3><p>Decentralization enables several important features that distinguish Web3 from its predecessor.</p><p>Web 3.0 technologies are</p><ul><li>Trustless</li><li>Verifiable</li><li>Permissionless</li><li>Crypto-native</li><li>Community-owned</li><li>Resistant to DDoS</li></ul><p>Of course, these advantages come at a cost. Smart contracts are slower and more expensive than a centralized backend. The UX of Web3 is also still far from perfect.</p><p>But also not every web app needs to be Web3.</p><h3 id="web-30-applications">Web 3.0 applications</h3><p>While elements of decentralization could be useful in a wide range of cases, Web3 also enables new unique applications.</p><p><a href="https://zerion.io/blog/how-to-invest-in-defi/"><strong>DeFi</strong></a> is probably the most striking example of Web3 applications. Smart contracts created a new alternative financial system, complete with trading, lending and borrowing, derivatives, and more.</p><p><strong>DAO </strong>or decentralized autonomous applications offer an alternative way of organizing people and work. Unlike traditional firms and other formal organizations, DAOs rely on code instead of law to create structures for cooperation. Instead of a bank account, a DAO can use a jointly managed crypto wallet. Instead of shares, DAO members can hold tokens and use them to vote for important decisions. Besides being Web 3.0 applications themselves, DAOs can also own other Web3 apps.</p><p><a href="https://zerion.io/blog/how-to-make-buy-and-sell-nfts/"><strong>NFTs</strong></a><strong> </strong>are not just expensive JPEGs, they can create a new alternative identity, outside of government IDs and profiles on Facebook or Google.</p><p><strong>GameFi</strong> apps can create new play-to-earn mechanics that merge play and work. Some games like Axies Infinity already give people in developing countries an opportunity to earn a living wage while playing.</p><p><strong>Metaverses</strong> can combine all those applications to create persistent virtual worlds, where a crypto wallet would act as a Web 3.0 passport and the holder of all assets, identities, and experiences.</p><p>Yet before these ambitious visions could come to fruition, a lot of missing blocks must be built. That’s what some of the blockchain projects are working on.</p><h2 id="web-30-blockchain-projects">Web 3.0 blockchain projects</h2><p>Web3 relies on a set of new decentralized technologies. Many of these projects have their own tokens that form the basis of their internal economies.</p><h3 id="popular-web-3-coins">Popular Web 3 coins</h3><p>Web 3.0 tokens are not a clearly defined category.
