<p>With great power comes great responsibility. In crypto, you can be a true owner of your assets and investments. But with self-custody, only you control your assets. No authority or power can return your crypto or reverse transactions. It’s up to you to protect yourself and your funds. In this guide, we’ll outline specific steps that you can take to keep crypto safe.</p><p><strong>Content:&nbsp;</strong></p><ol><li><a href="https://zerion.io/blog/how-to-stay-safe-in-crypto/#:~:text=1.-,Know%20your%20enemy,-First%2C%20you%20need" rel="noreferrer">Know your enemy</a></li><li><a href="https://zerion.io/blog/how-to-stay-safe-in-crypto/#:~:text=2.-,Choose%20the%20right%20crypto%20wallet,-You%20always%20make" rel="noreferrer">Choose the right crypto wallet</a></li><li><a href="https://zerion.io/blog/how-to-stay-safe-in-crypto/#:~:text=3.-,Use%20separate%20addresses,-It%E2%80%99s%20not%20wise" rel="noreferrer">Use separate addresses</a></li><li><a href="https://zerion.io/blog/how-to-stay-safe-in-crypto/#:~:text=4.-,Secure%20your%20seed%20phrase%20and%20private%20keys,-Regardless%20of%20what" rel="noreferrer">Secure your seed phrase and private keys</a></li><li><a href="https://zerion.io/blog/how-to-stay-safe-in-crypto/#:~:text=5.%20Know%20and%20avoid%20common%20scams" rel="noreferrer">Know and avoid common scams</a></li><li><a href="https://zerion.io/blog/how-to-stay-safe-in-crypto/#:~:text=6.-,Detect%20phishing,-The%20most%20likely" rel="noreferrer">Detect phishing</a></li><li><a href="https://zerion.io/blog/how-to-stay-safe-in-crypto/#:~:text=7.-,Understand%20your%20transactions,-Ok%2C%20you%20are" rel="noreferrer">Understand your transactions</a></li><li><a href="https://zerion.io/blog/how-to-stay-safe-in-crypto/#:~:text=Understand%20smart%20contract%20risks" rel="noreferrer">Understand smart contract risks</a></li><li><a href="https://zerion.io/blog/how-to-stay-safe-in-crypto/#:~:text=%C2%A09.-,Know%20where%20to%20get%20help%20if%20you%20need%20it,-Even%20if%20you" rel="noreferrer">Know where to get help if you need it</a></li></ol><h2 id="1-know-your-enemy">1. Know your enemy&nbsp;</h2><p>First, you need to understand who you’re up against.&nbsp;</p><p>Crypto criminals are in it strictly for the money. Like any business, they profit if their revenue is larger than costs.&nbsp;</p><p>Hackers will go after the <strong>biggest targets</strong>: exchanges, protocols, influencers, and whales. These attacks can be complicated, multi-step operations. But they will only target you (the user) personally if you are an influencer, a whale, or work for an exchange or protocol.&nbsp;</p><p>Alternatively, attackers might cast the net as widely as possible, aiming for lots of <strong>small targets</strong>. Attacks of this type would be automated or deployed at scale. Nobody will try to hack your wallet. They won’t even know its address. But they will try to trick you into taking an action that they want.</p><p>In this case, your own emotions are the biggest threat — scammers will try to use your greed and fear.</p><p>If you choose the right non-custodial wallet and simply store crypto, your assets will be safe in most cases. </p><h2 id="2-choose-the-right-crypto-wallet">2. Choose the right crypto wallet</h2><p>You always make a tradeoff between security and convenience.&nbsp;</p><p>An offline, off-grid nuclear bunker with a seed phrase on titanium plates could be the safest way to store your crypto. But then you can’t do much with it.</p><p>On the other spectrum are hot crypto wallets on your phone. Mobile wallets are the most convenient way to use crypto: you can sign any transaction on the go, trade, send, mint, and more. Its software isn’t the biggest risk. Instead, the convenience means you’ll make more transactions, some of which could lead to scams.</p><p>And there are lots of options in between, each with advantages and disadvantages:&nbsp;</p><ul><li><strong>Custodial wallet </strong>— any exchange wallet offers convenient conversion to and from fiat, but not your keys, not your coin. An exchange or another custodian can freeze your account or block withdrawals. Exchanges can also get hacked like the infamous Mt Gox or go bankrupt like FTX.</li><li><strong>Cold wallet</strong> — hardware wallets like <a href="https://www.ledger.com/">Ledger</a> securely store your private keys on a separate device, but you need to have it with you to make any transaction.</li><li><strong>Multisig wallet</strong> — smart contract wallets like <a href="https://safe.global/">Safe</a> require signatures by several keys to make transactions. This requirement increases safety but demands extra time for signing messages if you have keys on several devices.</li></ul><p>So, which crypto wallet is the best?&nbsp;</p><p>The best wallet depends on what you do with crypto.&nbsp;</p><p>However, it’s clear that to be an active Web3 citizen and stay safe, you need crypto wallets that are:</p><ol><li><strong>Self-custodial</strong> — only you control your private keys;</li><li><strong>Audited</strong> — reputable security auditors regularly check the code;</li><li><strong>Open source</strong> — the core code is available for anyone to explore, increasing the likelihood that any vulnerability will be reported.</li></ol><div class="kg-card kg-callout-card kg-callout-card-blue"><div class="kg-callout-emoji">✅</div><div class="kg-callout-text"><a href="https://link.zerion.io/2WyblS7WkFb" rel="noreferrer"><b><strong style="white-space: pre-wrap;">Zerion Wallet</strong></b></a> is exactly that: self-custodial, audited, and open-source. Plus, it has a number of <a href="https://zerion.io/security" rel="noreferrer">built-in security features</a>. </div></div><p>Still, don’t keep all eggs in one basket. Use several addresses, each for its purpose.</p><h2 id="3-use-separate-addresses">3. Use separate addresses</h2><p>It’s not wise to connect an address with all your life savings to a random dapp you see on Twitter.</p><p>Instead, use several addresses for different purposes:</p><ul><li><strong>Storing crypto and NFTs long-term</strong> — ideally, on cold wallets (aka hardware wallets) or an address you don’t regularly use online.&nbsp;</li><li><strong>Using DeFi</strong> — a desktop wallet, potentially a cold wallet, and multisig. You’ll make transactions to manage your portfolio, but probably once a month or once a week, not several times a day.&nbsp;</li><li><strong>Using Web3 social</strong> — an address with your ENS, Lens, and other handles to be used on both mobile and desktop wallets. Secure your seed phrase and private keys</h2><p>Regardless of what non-custodial wallet you use, you must securely store your seed phrase.</p><p>Account abstraction, social recovery, and other ways of creating and managing non-custodial wallets might eventually remove the need for seed phrase management. For now, those projects are arguably too early to store a meaningful amount of crypto.&nbsp;</p><p>So you’re stuck with seed phrases for now.</p><h3 id="how-not-to-store-the-seed-phrase">How NOT to store the seed phrase</h3><div class="kg-card kg-callout-card kg-callout-card-yellow"><div class="kg-callout-emoji">⚠️</div><div class="kg-callout-text">Even if you’ve heard this, it’s worth repeating where you should NOT store your seed phrase:</div></div><ul><li>Anywhere online in an unencrypted format (for example, in any note-taking app)</li><li>As a file or screenshot on your phone or computer</li><li>In a filing cabinet or other obvious physical storage places</li><li>As a paper note in your physical wallet&nbsp;</li></ul><p>In any of these places, there is a slight chance that someone can find your seed phrase. Then it’s game over.&nbsp;</p><p>One of the safest ways to store a seed phrase is in a fireproof safe or at least a secret stash. Besides protecting your seed phrase from criminals, consider how the seed can withstand fires, floods, or other natural disasters.</p><p>There is also a less extreme way to store your seed phrase.&nbsp;</p><h3 id="icloud-drive-backup-for-seed-phrase">iCloud / Drive backup for seed phrase</h3><p>Losing a physical copy of your seed phase is an underestimated risk.</p><p>If you have ever lost your password to Google or Facebook, you know that it can be a disaster. New scams come up every day.&nbsp;</p><p>All “mass-market” scams, aimed at small targets, try to get you to do one of three things:</p><ul><li>Send crypto</li><li>Share your seed phrase or private key</li><li>Sign a malicious transaction&nbsp;</li></ul><p>To trick you into one of these actions, scammers will likely use email, social media, or other scalable channels to start the attack.</p><h3 id="where-you-can-see-crypto-scams">Where you can see crypto scams</h3><p>You can come across crypto scams anywhere online:</p><ul><li>Spam email if you registered anywhere in compromised crypto services/newsletters</li><li>Twitter DMs and replies with giveaways, airdrops, exclusive mints, etc.</li><li>Google ads for keywords like Uniswap or Zerion, leading you to a fake page</li><li>Spam NFTs deposited into your address</li><li>Spam onchain transactions that make it look like an airdrop&nbsp;</li></ul><p>There is a common thread across all crypto scams: they use greed and FOMO. If something looks too good, it likely is a scam.&nbsp;</p><h3 id="how-to-avoid-scams">How to avoid scams</h3><p>Unfortunately, there is almost no way to avoid scams completely.&nbsp;</p><p>Even if you don’t use social media, email, and Google, scammers can still send you unwanted NFTs, tokens, and crypto transactions. Their aim is to get you to visit their website.</p><p>Luckily, <a href="https://link.zerion.io/2WyblS7WkFb" rel="noreferrer">Zerion Wallet</a> filters out a large chunk of onchain spam — it is hidden from your portfolio and transaction history.</p><h2 id="6-detect-phishing">6. Detect phishing</h2><p>The most likely crypto scam that you might encounter is a phishing website that will ask you to do one of those 3 actions: send crypto, share keys, sign a transaction.</p><p>There are two ways to protect yourself: recognize <a href="https://zerion.io/blog/crypto-phishing-what-it-is-and-how-zerion-can-help-to-protect-you/" rel="noreferrer">signs of crypto phishing</a> and automatically block known phishing domains.</p><h3 id="common-signs-of-phishing">Common signs of phishing</h3><p>Scammers usually do their best to make their phishing believable but they cannot make a fake site look 100% the real one.&nbsp;</p><p>Watch out for these signs:&nbsp;&nbsp;</p><ol><li><strong>Wrong URL</strong> — it could be subtle, like “l” instead of “i” or some non-Latin character</li><li><strong>Design</strong> — there could be small inconsistencies or issues that you normally won’t see on a real website</li><li><strong>Wording </strong>— the voice can be off-brand or the copy can contain mistakes&nbsp;</li></ol><p>If in doubt, share the link in the project’s Discord or with their support.&nbsp;</p><p>Luckily, many phishing domains are quickly reported, and your wallet can help you catch them.&nbsp;</p><h3 id="automatic-phishing-detection-in-zerion-wallet">Automatic phishing detection in Zerion Wallet</h3><p>When you try to connect to a website, Zerion Wallet will automatically check the URL against <a href="https://www.blockaid.io/post/emerging-from-stealth-with-33-m-in-funding-to-secure-web3"><u>Blockaid’s data</u></a>.&nbsp;</p>
